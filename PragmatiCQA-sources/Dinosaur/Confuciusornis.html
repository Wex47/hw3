Confuciusornis is a genus of primitive crow- sized from the Early-mid- and of , dating from 125 to 120 million years ago. Like modern birds, Confuciusornis had a toothless beak, but close relatives of modern birds such as and were toothed, indicating that the loss of teeth occurred convergently in Confuciusornis and living birds. It is the oldest known bird to have a beak. It was named after the Chinese moral philosopher (551–479 BCE). Confuciusornis is one of the most abundant vertebrates found in the Yixian Formation, and several hundred complete, articulated specimens have been found.

Description

C. sanctus fossil preserving long wing and tail feathers Confuciusornis was about the size of a modern crow, with a wingspan of up to 0.7 meters (2.3 ft) and a weight of up to 0.5 kilograms. Fossils of Confuciusornis show that it had an exceptionally large (upper arm bone), and a characteristic hole near its shoulder-end may have reduced the bone's weight. The or wishbone was a simple bar, like that of . The (breastbone) was relatively large and had a low which was raised at the back end. This bony keel may or may not have anchored a larger, cartilaginous, keel for enlarged muscles. The (shoulder blades) were fused to the bones and may have formed a solid base for the attachment of wing muscles. The orientation of the shoulder joint was sideways, instead of angled upward as in modern birds; this means that Confuciusornis was unable to lift its wings above its back. Like Archaeopteryx , it was thus incapable of the upstroke required for .

Confuciusornis was more advanced than Archaeopteryx in possessing a short tail with a (a bone formed from a series of short, fused tail vertebrae), but more primitive than modern birds in retaining large claws on the forelimbs, a primitive skull, and relatively small breastbone. The proportions of the toes suggest that they were used for both walking and perching, while the large claws of the thumb and third finger were probably used for climbing. The head probably had a small crest or tuft similar to that in today's or .

Paleobiology

Feathers and flight

Short tailed C. sanctus specimens in Cosmocaixa, BarcelonaThe wing feathers of Confuciusornis were long and modern in appearance. The primary wing feathers of a 0.5 kilogram individual reached 20.7 centimeters in length, about the same proportions as in the , which is similar in size and overall wingspan. The longest primary feathers ( ) were more than three times the length of the hand and relatively longer than those of any living bird, while the secondary feathers were rather short by comparison. Thus, the wing shape was very unlike that of living birds, being long and narrow. In 2010, Nudds and Dyke published a study arguing that in both Confuciusornis and , the rachis (central shaft) of the primary feathers were too thin and weak to have remained rigid during the power strokes required for true flight. Instead, they argued, Confuciusornis would have employed , which is also consistent with the unusual adaptations seen in its upper arm bones. , however, disagreed with their study. He argued that Nudds and Dyke had overestimated the weights of these early birds, and that more accurate weight estimates allowed powered flight even with relatively narrow rachises. Nudds and Dyke assumed a weight of 1.5 kilograms for Confuciusornis , as heavy as the modern . Paul argued that a more reasonable body weight estimate is about 180 grams, less than that of a . Paul also noted that Confuciusornis is commonly found as large assemblages in lake bottom sediments with little to no evidence of extensive postmortem transport, and that it would be highly unusual for gliding animals to be found in such large numbers in deep water. Rather, this evidence suggests that Confuciusornis traveled in large flocks over the lake surfaces, a habitat consistent with a flying animal.

Many individuals show long, streamer-like tail feathers that may indicate and could have been used in courtship display, but the rest of the tail feathers were small and probably of little use in flight.

Diet

There are immature specimens known, and from the analysis of bone growth patterns of young adults it has been estimated that Confuciusornis reached maturity somewhat slower than extant small birds, but faster than advanced dinosaurs (de Ricqlès et al. , 2003), which might indicate an omnivorous diet similar to modern .

It has been hypothesized that Confuciusornis fed on plant materials due to its toothless beak, but no or stomach contents had been reported (Zhou & Zhang, 2003). Dalsätt and colleagues (2006) described a specimen ( V133) that preserves 7 to 9 and several ribs of a fish (probably ). Classification and species

Recent cladistic analyses suggest that Confuciusornis is the most primitive bird; it has a more primitive skull than , but it is the first known bird to have lost the long tail of Archaeopteryx and develop fused tail vertebrae (a ). One controversial study concluded that Confuciusornis may be more closely related to and other dromaeosaurs than to Archaeopteryx , but this study was criticized on methodological grounds (Mayr et al. , 2005).

Six species have been formally classified: C. sanctus (the ), C. dui , C. feducciai , C. jianchangensis , C. chuonzhous and C. suniae (the latter two are usually considered synonymous with C. sanctus ).
